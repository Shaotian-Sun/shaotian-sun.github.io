## Introduction

_TASEP_ (Totally [Asymmetric Simple Exclusive Process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymmetric_simple_exclusion_process)) is of interest as it is one of the typical example in the so-called [_KPZ universality class_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kardar–Parisi–Zhang_equation). There is also an equaivalent relation between this process and _Exponential DLPP_ (Directed Last Passage Percolation), which is the main object that we have studied in REU.

In probability, people are curious to see whether or not a microscopic change will influence the macroscopic profile. In this blog, motivated by a meeting with Prof. Baik in the REU, I will talk about two different microscopic changes in TASEP and whether, and when they will have a macroscopic effect.

## TASEP

TASEP is describe an interacting particle system. Imagine there are holes in \\(\mathbb{Z}\\). For any of these hole, it can be filled with a particle or empty. All the particles, given the intial configuration, will move along the line of \\(\mathbb{Z}\\) with a time clock with rate \\(r\\). In other word, if the next position is empty, the waiting time for each particle is an exponential random variable with mean \\(\frac{1}{r}\\).

Typically, we let the rate \\(r=1\\), and study the initial configuration named _step initial condition_. That is when the particles are filled in \\(\mathbb{Z}\_{\le 0}\\).

Besides the KPZ universality class, TASEP also kind of models the trivial one-line traffic.

## 1st Microscopic Change: One Slow Bond

Now, instead of thinking of the clock assigned to each particle, we could give each integral point a clock: if it clicks, then we check if the current position is filled and the next one is empty. If so, we move the particle to the next.

For the _One Slow Bond_, the rate for the origin is \\(r\\), and \\(1\\) otherwise. (\\(r\le 1\\)) The question is that

$$
\text{Is there a critical value }r_c<1\text{ such that a sufficiently weak defect is macroscopically invisible?}
$$
